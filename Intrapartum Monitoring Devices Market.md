**Product Segmentation: Core Technologies in Intrapartum Surveillance**

**Monitors**

Modern monitors include both **external and internal fetal monitoring systems**, designed to track fetal heart rate (FHR) and uterine activity. Electronic Fetal Monitoring (EFM) systems are the cornerstone of labor surveillance. These are increasingly equipped with telemetry capabilities, allowing wireless monitoring that supports maternal movement and reduces labor complications. Portable monitors with Bluetooth or Wi-Fi connectivity are becoming common in both hospital and out-of-hospital settings.

**Electrodes**

Electrodes provide direct and highly accurate data acquisition. **Fetal scalp electrodes**, which attach to the fetal skin during active labor, are used when external monitoring is insufficient or unreliable. **Uterine Electromyography (EMG) electrodes** are utilized to detect the electrical activity of uterine muscles, offering deeper insights into contraction intensity and labor progression.

**Intrauterine Pressure Catheters (IUPC)**

These catheters are employed during advanced labor stages or when induction or augmentation is used. IUPCs offer real-time, quantitative assessments of uterine pressure and contraction patterns, making them indispensable tools in managing complex deliveries or suspected labor dysfunction.

**Transducers**

External Doppler-based transducers for FHR and tocodynamometers for uterine contractions are widely used, especially in hospital and maternity centers. These devices offer a non-invasive and real-time snapshot of labor progression and are often the first line of monitoring during initial labor stages.

**End-Use Segmentation: Diverse Clinical Settings**

**Hospitals**

Hospitals dominate market share due to their access to high-end monitoring infrastructure and skilled obstetricians. They employ continuous monitoring across labor and delivery units, operating rooms, and Neonatal Intensive Care Units (NICUs), where real-time alerts and electronic records are integrated into broader maternal-fetal care strategies.

**Maternity Centers**

These centers offer a hybrid approach, combining clinical-grade monitoring with comfort-focused, personalized care. They are growing in popularity due to their midwife-assisted model and use of compact, mobile monitoring systems that do not compromise clinical oversight.

**Other Settings**

This segment includes birthing homes, home-based labor monitoring supported by telemedicine, and mobile obstetric care units deployed in rural and conflict-affected regions. These setups demand robust, battery-powered, wireless monitoring systems that can deliver reliable data without the need for large infrastructure.
